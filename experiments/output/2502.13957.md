## Leveling Up RAG Agents with Process Supervision: Introducing RAG-Gym and ReSearch

Large Language Models (LLMs) have become incredibly powerful, revolutionizing how we access information online. They can answer questions directly and often possess vast general knowledge. However, they still struggle with complex knowledge-intensive tasks, especially those requiring multi-step reasoning and sequential information retrieval. Think of questions where you need to look up one fact to figure out what to search for next. Traditional Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) helps by grounding LLMs in external knowledge, but static, single-pass retrieval falls short for these multi-hop scenarios.

While recent work has explored agentic approaches where LLMs interact iteratively with search tools (like ReAct), these often rely heavily on delicate prompt engineering, which can be fragile and hard to generalize across tasks. What if we could train these agents to be better at *how* they search and reason at each step, not just rely on perfect prompting?

This is where RAG-Gym comes in. Introduced in a new paper, RAG-Gym is a unified optimization framework designed to enhance information-seeking agents by applying fine-grained *process supervision* at every step of their interaction with the environment. Instead of just rewarding the final correct answer, RAG-Gym focuses on evaluating and improving the quality of intermediate actions, like generating effective search queries or making sound reasoning steps.

RAG-Gym models the knowledge-intensive QA task as a nested Markov Decision Process (MDP). The 'outer' MDP handles the high-level actions – deciding whether to search or answer, and if searching, generating the query. The 'inner' MDP is the LLM's sequential token generation process that produces these actions. The core idea is to collect data on the quality of the outer MDP actions using process rewards. This process reward data is generated by evaluating candidate actions based on criteria like *Sufficiency* (Is searching needed? Or is there enough info to answer?), *Utility* (Is the query precise and helpful?), and *Redundancy* (Does the query seek new information?). This data can then be used to train the agent's policy using methods like Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), or by training a separate Process Reward Model (PRM) to act as a verifier during inference.

<br>![Figure 2: Architecture of ReSearch, which synergizes Reasoning and Search by integrating history knowledge summarization, answer reasoning, and query generation to iteratively resolve missing information in constructing the final answer.](../../test/tem/2502.13957_Figure2.png)<br>

Alongside the RAG-Gym framework, the paper proposes ReSearch, a novel agent architecture specifically designed for knowledge-intensive QA. Existing agents like ReAct often separate reasoning about the answer from generating a search query, which can lead to queries that aren't tightly aligned with the information needed to build the final answer. ReSearch unifies these processes. It first attempts to construct a potential answer based on the current information history. If it identifies claims in this potential answer that are not supported by evidence (unverified claims), it uses these specific knowledge gaps as the basis for generating the next search query. This "answer-driven" query generation ensures that each search is highly targeted at resolving uncertainties in the ongoing answer construction.

Experiments on challenging datasets like HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA, Bamboogle, and MedQA demonstrate the effectiveness of RAG-Gym and ReSearch. Process supervision consistently boosts the performance of various agent architectures, with PRM showing the most significant gains (up to 25.6% improvement on average F1 over zero-shot baselines for ReAct). ReSearch consistently outperforms other baseline agents, achieving state-of-the-art results, especially when optimized within RAG-Gym.

The research also explores interesting aspects of process supervision:
*   **LLMs as Judges:** Advanced LLMs like GPT-4o are shown to be highly effective and align well with human judgments for annotating process reward data, making scalable data collection feasible.
*   **Reward Model Transferability:** Process reward models trained on one LLM can effectively guide the action selection of a different LLM during inference, suggesting they can serve as plug-and-play modules to enhance proprietary models.
*   **Scaling Laws:** Performance generally improves with more training data and by sampling more actions during inference (when using PRM), but there are diminishing returns, indicating saturation points for both.

In summary, RAG-Gym provides a principled framework for optimizing agentic RAG through explicit process-level feedback, moving beyond prompt engineering. ReSearch offers a powerful, answer-driven architecture that is particularly well-suited for this process supervision approach. The findings highlight the potential of leveraging process rewards, even generated by LLMs, to train more capable and robust information-seeking agents for complex tasks.
