# GFM-RAG: Towards a Graph Foundation Model for Better Retrieval Augmented Generation

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities, but their knowledge is often static and limited by their training data. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a powerful paradigm to overcome this, allowing LLMs to access and incorporate real-time, external knowledge by retrieving relevant documents.

However, traditional RAG systems often retrieve documents in isolation, struggling to capture the intricate relationships *between* different pieces of information scattered across multiple sources. This limitation becomes particularly apparent in complex tasks like multi-hop question answering, where piecing together facts from various documents is crucial for accurate reasoning.

Graph-enhanced RAG (GraphRAG) attempts to address this by building graph structures (like Knowledge Graphs or KG-indexes) from documents to explicitly model these relationships. This allows retrievers to navigate the knowledge landscape more intelligently. While promising, existing GraphRAG methods often suffer from noise and incompleteness in the constructed graphs and, more critically, lack generalizability. They typically require training from scratch or extensive fine-tuning for each new dataset, hindering their widespread applicability.

This paper introduces **GFM-RAG**, a novel approach that aims to tackle these challenges by proposing the first **Graph Foundation Model (GFM)** specifically designed for retrieval augmented generation.

<br>![Figure 1: The overview framework of GFM-RAG. We feed the query and a constructed KG-index into the graph foundation model retriever to obtain relevant documents for LLM generation.](../test/tem/2502.01113_Figure1.png)<br>

At its core, GFM-RAG leverages a graph structure, a KG-index, built from the document corpus. This index links factual triples extracted from the documents, providing a structured representation of the knowledge within the corpus.

The key innovation lies in the **GFM Retriever**. Unlike traditional retrievers or graph-specific GNNs, the GFM Retriever is powered by an innovative **query-dependent Graph Neural Network**. This GNN is designed to reason over the graph structure while being guided by the user's query. By making the message passing and aggregation query-aware, it can dynamically identify entities and relationships most relevant to the specific question, effectively performing multi-hop reasoning across the graph in a single pass.

To achieve generalizability, the GFM Retriever, a model with 8 million parameters, undergoes a two-stage training process on a massive scale:
1.  **Unsupervised KG Completion Pre-training:** The model learns to reason about graph structure and complete missing information by training on a large collection of 60 KGs comprising over 14 million triples.
2.  **Supervised Document Retrieval Fine-tuning:** The model is then fine-tuned on a large dataset of 700,000 documents paired with queries, learning to retrieve relevant documents by leveraging the KG-index.

This large-scale, two-stage training equips the GFM Retriever with the ability to be applied directly to *unseen* datasets and KGs *without any additional fine-tuning*. This is the hallmark of a foundation model – its learned representations and reasoning capabilities transfer effectively to new tasks and domains.

Extensive experiments demonstrate GFM-RAG's impressive capabilities. On three multi-hop QA datasets (HotpotQA, MuSiQue, 2Wiki), GFM-RAG achieves state-of-the-art retrieval and question answering performance, significantly outperforming previous methods, including complex multi-step retrieval pipelines. This highlights its effectiveness and efficiency in complex reasoning tasks.

<br>![Figure 3: Model performance and generalizability comparison. Retrieval and reasoning with LLMs. In contrast, GFM-RAG conducts multi-hop reasoning within a single-step GNN reasoning, which is more effective than single-step methods and more efficient than multi-step ones.](../test/tem/2502.01113_Figure3.png)<br>

More importantly, GFM-RAG showcases strong generalizability. Evaluated on seven domain-specific RAG datasets spanning biomedical, customer support, and general knowledge domains, the *zero-shot* performance of GFM-RAG (without any fine-tuning on these datasets) surpasses state-of-the-art graph-enhanced methods like HippoRAG. This confirms its potential as a versatile foundation model for RAG.

Furthermore, the paper investigates the neural scaling laws of GFM-RAG, observing that its performance improves predictably with increases in both training data size and model parameter count.

<br>![Figure 4: Neural scaling law of GFM-RAG.](../test/tem/2502.01113_Figure4.png)<br>

This adherence to scaling laws suggests that GFM-RAG's capabilities can be further enhanced by scaling up training data and model size in the future, similar to the trajectory of large language models.

In summary, GFM-RAG introduces a significant step towards building foundation models for graph-enhanced RAG. By coupling a query-dependent GNN with large-scale, multi-task training on KGs and documents, it offers a powerful, efficient, and generalizable solution for integrating complex knowledge into LLMs, paving the way for more capable and flexible RAG systems.
